AI / EV Technology

How AI Can Predict EV Battery Failures

Modern EV batteries generate thousands of data points per second — voltage spikes, temperature gradients, micro-vibrations. Most systems discard this data. AI doesn't. By training ML models on historical sensor streams, we can identify failure signatures weeks before a breakdown occurs. This isn't theoretical: real-time predictive analytics is already reducing downtime and improving safety in production EV systems. The goal isn't just detection — it's prevention, and that changes everything about how we think about battery reliability.

04
AI Strategy / Industry Analysis

Where Is AI Actually Winning? A Domain-by-Domain Breakdown

Everyone says AI is changing everything. But look closer, and you'll notice something uncomfortable: it's changing some things dramatically and barely touching others. The gap between what AI can theoretically do and what's actually deployed in production isn't uniform — it's wildly uneven across domains.

The chart above maps Theoretical AI Capability against Current Real-World Deployment across nine domains. In AI/ML, Data Science, and Content/GenAI, the gap is nearly closed. These domains have one thing in common: low stakes for being wrong. A hallucinated sentence is annoying. A hallucinated firewall rule is catastrophic.

That's exactly why Backend infrastructure, Cybersecurity, and Systems engineering still show the largest deployment gaps. The moment AI touches critical pipelines, trust becomes the bottleneck — not capability. Reliability, auditability, and infrastructure complexity are walls that benchmark scores alone don't break through.

AI isn't replacing domains equally — it's replacing tasks within domains selectively. It's eating the predictable, repeatable, high-volume tasks first. The creative, the judgment-heavy, the high-stakes? Those are proving far more resilient than the headlines suggest.

As someone building AI systems — not just studying them — I've felt this firsthand. Training a model is the easy part. Deploying it into a system where it has to be fast, correct, explainable, and recoverable under failure? That's a completely different discipline. Deployment isn't the finish line. It's where the real engineering begins.

"The future won't be built by people who only understand AI — but by those who understand where AI still fails."

Career / AI Displacement / Personal

'Half of These Roles May Not Exist in 5 Years.'

A Monday morning at L&T. A quiet room. And a question no one wanted to answer out loud.

Our HR said it without warning. No slide, no build-up — just a sentence dropped into a Monday morning briefing like it belonged there.

"Half of these roles may not exist in 5 years."

The room didn't react dramatically. That was the unsettling part. Engineers, analysts, developers — everyone just did the same quiet calculation behind their eyes. Am I on the list?

I had my coffee in my hand. Put it down. Picked it up again. Finished it in one sip. Then opened my laptop — because that's the only response that made sense to me.

I spent the next hour going through industry reports, adoption studies, and real deployment data. Not the headline version. The actual numbers. And then I built a spider chart comparing where AI theory stands versus where it's actually deployed across tech domains.

Here's what the data said, without the noise: AI is closing the gap fast in AI/ML, Data Science, and Content Generation. The tools exist. The products ship. The adoption is real. But Backend Engineering, Cybersecurity, and complex systems infrastructure? The gap is still wide — and it isn't closing at the same pace. Not because AI isn't capable of more. Because replacing critical systems requires something capability alone can't buy: trust, regulation, and time.

The real threat isn't AI taking your job tomorrow morning. It's staying exactly where you are while the market quietly moves in a different direction. That gap — between your current position and where the industry is going — that's the one worth worrying about.

I'm 19. Still finishing my degree. Interning at L&T on real AI systems, not classroom assignments. And I can already see this shift — not in think-pieces or conference keynotes, but in actual offices, in actual decisions, in the way people pause before answering certain questions.

If I can see it from here, imagine what the people making hiring decisions already know.

The choice ahead isn't complicated, but it is binary: either you move into AI, or you build AI into your field. The middle ground — waiting and watching — is shrinking faster than most people are comfortable admitting.

"The question was never whether AI would change your industry. It's whether you'll be the one shaping that change — or the one it happens to."

Enterprise Computing / Systems Engineering

Why Mainframes Still Run the World (And Why Everyone Is Ignoring Them)

While everyone is talking about AI, the systems running the world are still mainframes.

The reality is uncomfortable for the tech hype cycle: banking, global payments, airlines, and government infrastructure don't run on the latest trendy framework. They run on enterprise mainframes like IBM Z. When processing billions of transactions securely, without a single millisecond of downtime, stability overwhelmingly beats hype.

AI is undeniably powerful. But AI models don't exist in a vacuum — they require an incredibly resilient, high-bandwidth, and fault-tolerant infrastructure to actually function at scale. Mainframes are the silent backbone that keeps this global data flowing.

This exposes a critical insight: Systems engineering is far more critical than just model building. Creating an AI prototype is one thing. Ensuring its supporting infrastructure handles extreme scale, guarantees uptime, and provides absolute fault tolerance is a completely different discipline.

As an AI/ML student, I realized something important: building models is surprisingly easier than deploying and maintaining them at a global scale. This is exactly why enterprise systems matter. You can't scale the future on fragile foundations.

"Everyone wants to build AI. Few want to understand what keeps it alive. The future won't just be built by AI engineers — but by engineers who understand the systems that keep AI running."

07
Enterprise Computing / In-Transaction AI

Zero-Latency AI: Why I'm Building on IBM Z

The standard AI playbook is broken for high-stakes transactions. Usually, an event occurs, data is sent across a network to a cloud server, the AI model runs inference, and the result is sent back. For an e-commerce recommendation, that 200-millisecond delay is fine.

For a credit card swipe, 200 milliseconds is an eternity.

This is why I'm moving beyond traditional cloud deployments and focusing on IBM Z and the Telum processor. Instead of sending data to the AI, IBM Z brings the AI directly to the data.

By leveraging on-chip AI acceleration, we can achieve in-transaction scoring. This means running complex deep learning models to detect fraud during the actual transaction window — in under 1 millisecond. No network hops. No API latency. Just pure, immediate compute.

It's a complete paradigm shift. As an engineer, deploying an ONNX model directly into a z/OS pipeline teaches you that the ultimate bottleneck isn't usually the algorithm's complexity — it's the infrastructure's physics.

"True scale isn't just about handling billions of requests. It's about handling them so fast that the AI is invisible."
